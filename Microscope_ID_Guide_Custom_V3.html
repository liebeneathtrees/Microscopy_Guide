What is Meiofauna?

Meiofauna (or meiobenthos) are microscopic metazoans defined by their size range: organisms that pass through a 1000μm (1mm) sieve but are retained on a 40μm (0.04mm) sieve. For this guide, we extend the upper limit to 2000μm (2mm) to include juvenile stages and larger species. This size class represents the smallest metazoan community in aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems.

📏 Size Classification:
40μm - 2000μm (0.04mm - 2mm)
Roughly the size range from the width of a fine human hair to the thickness of a dry strand of spaghetti

Habitat Types & Ecological Niches

  • Planktic: Free-swimming in the water column (rotifers, copepod nauplii, cladocerans)
  • Epiphytic: Living on plant surfaces, macrophytes, and moss (gastrotrichs, rotifers, tardigrades)
  • Epibenthic: On sediment surface or attached to rocks in biofilms (diatoms, stalked protists, some nematodes)
  • Interstitial: Living between sediment particles in sand and gravel (many nematodes, harpacticoid copepods, ostracods)
  • Burrowing: Actively moving through fine sediments like mud and silt (oligochaetes, some chironomids)
  • Terrestrial: Soil, leaf litter, and moss habitats (collembola, many nematodes, tardigrades)

🧰 Essential Equipment

Microscopy Equipment

  • Stereo/Dissecting Microscope (10x-80x): For sorting, initial identification, and larger organisms. Essential for field work and preliminary examination
  • Compound Microscope (40x-400x): For detailed identification of smaller organisms and fine morphological features. Phase contrast capability highly recommended
  • Slides & Coverslips: Depression/concave slides for living specimens; flat slides for pressed/mounted specimens
  • Petri Dishes: Glass or plastic, 60-90mm diameter for aqueous samples, sorting, and long-term observation

Sieving Equipment (Critical)

Sieves separate meiofauna from sediments and debris by size. Multi-sieve stacks improve separation:

  • 500μm sieve: Removes macrofauna and large debris (upper limit for meiofauna)
  • 125μm sieve: Acceptable for classroom exercises; retains most larger meiofauna
  • 40μm sieve: Standard research sieve; retains vast majority of meiofauna
  • 10μm sieve: Captures the very smallest protists and fine organic particles; rarely needed for routine meiofauna work
💡 Pro Tip: Sieves can be economically made from Nitex mesh and PVC pipe or plastic jars. Cut PVC drain pipe (8-10cm diameter) into 12-14cm lengths, secure Nitex mesh with PVC couplers

Field Sampling Devices

  • Plankton Net (63μm mesh): Tow net for open water sampling of planktonic rotifers, cladocerans, copepods, and protists. Use with a collection jar at the cod end
  • Corers: PVC or acrylic pipe (various diameters) for collecting intact sediment cores. Cut-off 30mL syringes work for shallow sampling
  • Bou-Rouch Pump: Hand-operated bilge pump + perforated wellpoint for deep gravel/cobble beds
  • Baermann Funnel: Heat-gradient extraction device for moss, vegetation, and organic substrates
  • Brush-Sampler: Toothbrush mounted on syringe piston for scraping biofilms from defined areas
  • Turkey Baster/Pipette: For sampling surface muds and floating organisms

Preservation & Handling Supplies

  • 6% MgCl₂ solution (73.2 g/L): Anesthetic to relax specimens before fixation
  • 10% Buffered Formalin + Rose Bengal: Standard preservative with protein stain (1 g/L Rose Bengal)
  • Ethanol (70-95%): Alternative fixative; better for DNA work and soft-bodied organisms
  • Glycerol: For clearing specimens and making permanent slides
  • Sample containers: Plastic jars, vials, zip-lock bags for field collection
  • Wash bottles, pipettes, worm pickers, mouth pipettes for specimen manipulation

🧪 Sampling Methods by Habitat Type

1. Planktonic Organisms (Open Water)

A. Plankton Net Tow

Standard method for open water sampling:

  1. Use plankton net (63μm mesh for microplankton)
  2. Tow vertically or horizontally through water column
  3. Record distance towed for quantitative samples
  4. Rinse organisms from net into collection container
  5. Preserve immediately or keep cool for live observation
B. Water Column Sampling

For small water bodies without net:

  1. Collect 5-10L water from mid-depth
  2. Concentrate through plankton net or fine sieve (40-63μm)
  3. Multiple tows increase specimen diversity
  4. Sample different times of day for daily migrators

2. Vegetation, Moss & Organic Substrates

A. Baermann Funnel Extraction

Heat-gradient extraction for moss, leaf litter, terrestrial samples:

  1. Set up funnel apparatus: glass funnel + rubber tubing with pinch clamp
  2. Fill funnel with room temperature water
  3. Place sample on fine mesh screen or gauze suspended in water
  4. Position desk lamp above sample (heat source)
  5. Wait 24-48 hours for organisms to migrate down
  6. Collect concentrated organisms from tube bottom
💡 Pro Tip: Avoid overheating - maintain water at 25-30°C. Tardigrades, nematodes, and rotifers migrate readily with this method
B. Macrophyte Shaking/Rinsing

For epiphytic fauna on aquatic plants:

  1. Collect plant material carefully to avoid dislodging organisms
  2. Place in container with habitat water
  3. Shake vigorously for 2-3 minutes
  4. Remove plant material carefully
  5. Pour water through appropriate sieve (40μm or 125μm)
  6. Examine retained material

3. Coarse Sediment (Sand, Gravel, Cobbles)

A. Swirl-Decant Procedure

Best for qualitative or quantitative extraction from coarse substrates:

  1. Remove sediment sample (shovel or core) and place in 10L bucket
  2. Add 5-6L filtered stream/habitat water
  3. Stir vigorously to suspend fine particles and organisms
  4. Wait 2-3 seconds for heavy particles to settle
  5. Pour supernatant through 40μm sieve (or sieve stack)
  6. Repeat 5-6 times to extract most organisms
  7. Rinse retained material from sieve into sample container
B. Coring Method

For quantitative sampling of interstitial fauna:

  1. Stand downstream of sampling site to avoid disturbance
  2. Insert corer vertically into sediment (10cm+ depth for sand/gravel)
  3. For fine sediment: cork the top before withdrawing to prevent sample loss
  4. For coarse sediment: place hand under core bottom before lifting
  5. Extract core and process immediately or preserve
  6. For vertical distribution: slice cores at specific depth intervals
C. Bou-Rouch Pump Sampling

For deep gravel/cobble beds or groundwater fauna:

  1. Drive perforated wellpoint pipe into sediment with sledgehammer
  2. Reach desired depth (deepest 15cm of pipe should be perforated)
  3. Attach hand-operated bilge pump to pipe
  4. Pump interstitial water through sieve series
  5. Collect 10-20L per sample point
⚠️ Note: Pump sampling may damage delicate organisms and gives variable results based on sediment permeability

4. Biofilm & Epilithic Organisms (Rock Surfaces)

A. Cobble Scraping Method

For periphyton and biofilm-dwelling meiofauna:

  1. Select representative cobbles from riffle areas
  2. Place cobble in ziplock bag or container with habitat water
  3. Scrub cobble surface with toothbrush (quantitative: 10cm² template)
  4. Measure surface area (tin foil method or geometric calculation)
  5. Concentrate scrubbed material through sieve
  6. Rinse thoroughly to capture all organisms
B. In Situ Brush-Sampler

For quantitative periphyton sampling:

  1. Position PVC cylinder (known area) on rock surface
  2. Insert toothbrush mounted on syringe piston
  3. Scrub defined area thoroughly (50 strokes)
  4. Extract material with pipette or syringe
  5. Preserve or examine immediately

🧪 Sample Preservation Protocols

Standard Formalin Preservation

  1. Anesthetize specimens first: Add 6% MgCl₂ solution gradually over 10-15 minutes to relax organisms
  2. Fix immediately: Add 10% buffered formalin (final concentration 4-5%)
  3. Add Rose Bengal: Protein stain (1 g/L) helps locate specimens in debris
  4. Label clearly: Include location, date, habitat type, collector name
  5. Storage: Samples stable for years in formalin
⚠️ Safety: Formalin is toxic and carcinogenic. Work in ventilated area, use gloves, dispose properly

Ethanol Preservation (DNA Work)

For molecular work or soft-bodied organisms:

  • Use 70-95% ethanol (no anesthetic needed)
  • Change ethanol after 24 hours for better preservation
  • Better for tardigrades, nematodes, rotifers
  • Essential for DNA extraction and sequencing

Glycerol Mounting (Permanent Slides)

For long-term microscope slide preservation:

  1. Transfer specimens from preservative to water
  2. Transfer to 50% glycerol gradually
  3. Transfer to pure glycerol
  4. Mount on slide, seal coverslip with nail polish
  5. Clears specimens and allows detailed observation

✅ Best Practices & Safety

  • Label everything: Write labels in pencil on waterproof paper, include inside container
  • Keep samples cool: Ice chest in field, refrigerate if not preserving immediately
  • Process quickly: Meiofauna stress depletes oxygen; process within hours if possible
  • Standardize methods: Use consistent techniques for comparable results
  • Record metadata: Habitat type, water parameters, weather, substrate composition
  • Replicate samples: Take 3-5 replicates per site for robust data
  • Safety first: Wear waders in streams, beware of currents, use gloves with chemicals
  • Permits: Obtain necessary permits for protected areas or species
